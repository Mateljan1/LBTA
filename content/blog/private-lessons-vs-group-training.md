There is no trophy answer. Private lessons and group training solve different problems. Most committed players use both — just not always in the same month.

## Private lessons: precision and diagnosis

One-on-one time is strongest when you need **rapid correction**, a technical rebuild, or a quiet space to work through a single pattern (serve rhythm, return positioning, transition footwork). The coach sees every swing; feedback density is high. For some adults and younger juniors, that clarity is the difference between frustration and progress.

Cost and scheduling matter. If privates are rare, use them with a narrow goal you can rehearse in homework or [open practice](/schedules).

## Group training: pressure, variety, and reps

Groups teach you to **hit on the move**, read different balls, and manage energy when the basket is not yours alone. Doubles-based work, live-ball drills, and match-play blocks belong here. Many players discover their real level in a group — where unpredictability shows up the way it does on weekend play.

## The usual mix

A common rhythm: a **group core** for volume and tactics, plus **private touchpoints** when something breaks or before a competitive stretch. Juniors in pathway programs often live mostly in groups during the semester and add privates around specific goals; adults might invert that if their schedule prefers small-group clinics.
