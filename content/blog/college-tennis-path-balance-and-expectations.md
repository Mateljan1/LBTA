The college conversation in tennis often arrives too hot: rankings, videos, showcases, and a quiet panic that someone else is “ahead.” Most programs are looking for players who can **compete** and **graduate** — and for families who understand what four years actually require.

## Tennis is one part of a whole application

Even strong UTR or national results do not replace transcript and testing expectations set by each school. Start with academic fit and health — then layer tennis. That order saves time and protects the player when form dips or injury appears.

## What coaches actually watch

Beyond outcomes, many coaches notice **how** a player problem-solves between points, how they recover physically in long matches, and how they treat partners, opponents, and staff. Your highlight reel matters; your patterns under stress matter at least as much.

## The high-performance lane is not for everyone — by design

Our [high-performance pathway](/programs/high-performance) is invitation-only because the training load and travel assumptions are serious. That is not a judgment on love for the game; it is a match between readiness and demands. Plenty of players thrive in structured [junior development](/programs/junior) and [adult progression](/programs/adult) without chasing a scholarship.

## Keep the player’s voice in the room

If the process starts to feel like a job in sophomore year, reassess volume and rest. A sustainable path almost always beats a heroic sprint that empties the tank before campus.

---

College tennis can be a remarkable chapter. Build the person, the student, and the competitor in parallel — and let the right fit find you.
