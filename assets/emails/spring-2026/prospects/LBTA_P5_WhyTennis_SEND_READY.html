What the Court Teaches.

3 things tennis builds that transfer to everything else

Hey %FIRSTNAME%,

I’ve taught tennis for over 15 years — internationally and here in Laguna Beach. I’ve coached kids who went on to play college tennis and kids who quit after a year. I’ve coached adults who started at 25 and adults who picked it up at 60.

The one thing I’m most certain of is this: tennis teaches three things that almost no other environment can.

Decision-making under pressure. In a team sport, a coach can call a timeout and tell you what to do. In tennis, you’re alone on the court. Every ball requires a decision — where to hit, how hard, where to move next — and you have less than a second to make it. There is no huddle. That kind of rapid, independent decision-making transfers to everything.

Emotional regulation. You’re going to miss shots. You’re going to make bad decisions. You’re going to be down in a match and feel like it’s slipping away. What tennis teaches is the ability to reset — to let go of the last point and focus on the next one. That skill is worth more than any forehand.

Competitive grace. Winning without gloating. Losing without excuses. Shaking hands after a tough match and meaning it. These aren’t things you can lecture a child about. They’re things they have to practice — and tennis provides that practice every single session.

These aren’t bonus features of the sport. For me, they’re the reason the sport matters.
