What a class actually looks like — from warm-up to parent handoff

Hey %FIRSTNAME%,

The biggest barrier to trying something new isn’t the cost. It’s the unknown.

What happens when my kid shows up? Will they know anyone? Will they be the worst one there? What if they don’t like it?

I hear some version of this from almost every new parent. So I want to take the mystery out of it. Here’s what a real 60-minute Red Ball session looks like — the kind your 5- or 6-year-old would walk into.

INSIDE A 60-MINUTE SESSION

Minutes 0–10: Warm-up games. Not stretching. Not laps. Tag with tennis balls. Relay races between cones. By minute 10, every kid is smiling and slightly out of breath.

Minutes 10–25: Skill game. Coach Allison sets up a game with specific rules that develop a specific skill. If the theme this week is “rally tolerance,” the game might be: keep the ball going 5 times to earn a point. No lecture on grip. Just a target, a rule, and a reason to try.

Minutes 25–40: Rally game. Partners work together, then compete. Maybe only crosscourt shots count. Maybe bonus points for deep balls. The rules shape the behavior without the coach saying a word.

Minutes 40–50: Real points. Champions and challengers. The energy goes up. This is where kids learn to compete — to win with grace and lose without falling apart.

Minutes 50–55: Cool-down game. Something fun and low-pressure. Coach gives one specific compliment to each kid — not “good job” but “your recovery step after every shot today was strong.”

Minutes 55–60: Parent handoff. Coach is available for a quick word. A sentence or two on what they worked on and what went well.

No standing in line waiting for a turn. No 20-minute lectures on technique. Just 60 minutes of structured, purposeful play that happens to build real tennis skills along the way.

One thing you might notice: our coaches use targets and cones instead of verbal corrections. “Aim through the gate” works better than “follow through higher.” External cues — things you can see and aim at — produce better skill retention than internal body-part instructions. That’s not just our opinion. It’s what the motor learning research consistently shows.
