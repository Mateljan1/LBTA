The 70% Rule.

Why the right difficulty level matters more than more reps

Hey %FIRSTNAME%,

There’s a concept in motor learning research called desirable difficulty — and it explains something most tennis players get wrong about practice.

If you’re hitting every ball in during a drill, you’re not improving. You’re just reinforcing what you already know. And if you’re missing everything, you’re just frustrated. Neither one moves the needle.

The sweet spot — where learning actually happens fastest — is around 70% success. Hard enough that you have to focus. Easy enough that you can feel yourself getting better. That tension between challenge and confidence is where real growth lives.

This is how we calibrate every drill at LBTA. We adjust targets, distances, and rules until the success rate feels like “I can do this, but I have to concentrate.” If kids are nailing everything, we make the target smaller or add a constraint. If they’re struggling, we pull back. The 70% zone is the goal.

And this is why concentrated volume — like a week of camp — creates breakthroughs that weekly classes can’t always match. Four days in a row at 70% difficulty gives the brain something a once-a-week session can’t: enough repetition in a short window for the skill to actually click. The learning compounds.

THE PRINCIPLE — DESIRABLE DIFFICULTY

Next time you practice — or watch your child practice — pay attention to the success rate. If they’re making everything, the drill is too easy. If they’re missing everything, it’s too hard.

The zone you’re looking for: make about 7 out of 10. That’s where the brain is working hard enough to adapt but succeeding often enough to build confidence.

Try it this week: If you’re doing a serving drill, find the target size where you hit 7 out of 10. That’s your training zone. If you’re hitting 10 out of 10, the target is too big. Move it or shrink it.
