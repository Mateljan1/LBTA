# LBTA Team Launch Announcement

> The message Andrew sends to Allison and Saska on Day 1 of the rollout. Calm, confident, no hype. Frames the GPTs as **a tool, not a system change** — they still own the work, the GPTs just hand them better starting points.

---

## Send via: text or in-person, NOT email.

The medium matters. An email reads as a policy change. A text or hallway conversation reads as a tool offer.

## Allison — text version (180 words)

> Hey — quick one. I've spent a few weeks building a GPT for you that drafts your member emails, league recaps, and weekly clinic notes in your voice. It's loaded with everything that's on the website (programs, prices, schedule, policies) and pulls from your past emails so it sounds like you, not a robot.
>
> Here's the deal: you still own everything. You read every draft, edit if it's off, and you ship. The GPT just gives you a 70% draft instead of a blank page. It saves the typing, not the thinking.
>
> Thursday morning, 20 minutes, I'll walk you through it. We'll ship one real member email together so you trust it. After that, use it however much (or little) makes sense for you.
>
> Friday afternoon at 4, we'll do a 15-min check-in to talk through what worked and what didn't. That's the whole rhythm. No new meetings beyond that.
>
> Two questions for you: what's the worst kind of email to write right now, and what's a draft that always sounds great when *you* write it (so I can teach the GPT)?

## Saska — text version (180 words)

> Hey — I've spent some time building a GPT just for the junior side. It drafts parent emails, weekly group recaps, and camp comms in your voice. Loaded with everything from the website (camps, schedules, coach info) and tuned for the parent-of-a-kid register, which is different from adult member comms.
>
> The rule is simple: you still own everything. You read every draft, edit anything off, and you ship. The GPT just hands you a starting point so you're not staring at a blank email at 9pm.
>
> Friday morning, 20 minutes, I'll show you how it works. We'll ship one real parent email together. After that, you decide how much you use it.
>
> Friday afternoons at 4, 15-min check-in. That's the only new meeting. We talk about what worked, what didn't, I tighten the GPT for next week.
>
> Two things for me: what kind of parent message takes you the longest right now, and a recent email of yours you're proud of (so I can teach it your voice)?

## In-person variant (60 seconds, if you're seeing them on court)

> Hey — built you a GPT. Drafts your [member emails / parent emails], in your voice. You still ship everything. We'll do 20 min Thursday/Friday morning to set it up. 15 min Friday at 4 to tune. That's it. Cool?

## What to NOT say (yet)

- Don't say "this will save you 5 hours a week." (Set expectations, don't oversell.)
- Don't say "we're rolling this out across the academy." (It's two coaches, calmly.)
- Don't say "AI is the future." (Brand voice, not Silicon Valley voice.)
- Don't say "you'll love it." (Let them decide.)
- Don't promise it'll work on Day 1. (It won't on every email. That's why Friday review exists.)

## What to say if they push back

- "Will it sound like me?" → "Yes — that's what the past-emails part is for. If it doesn't, we fix it Friday."
- "Will I have to learn AI stuff?" → "No. You read drafts, you edit, you ship. Same as today, just with a better starting point."
- "What if it makes a mistake?" → "It will. You'll catch it. We log it Friday and fix the GPT for next week."
- "Do I have to use it?" → "No. It's a tool. Use it where it helps. Skip where it doesn't."

## Three days later (follow-up text)

> How's it going? Anything specific the GPT has helped with, or anything off?

If they say "not much" → not a problem. Wait for Friday.
If they say "voice is off" → fix it Friday, or sooner if it's hot.
If they say "love it" → great, but don't celebrate yet. Wait for week 2.

## After Day 28 (one month in)

If they're using it 5x/week and shipping with light edits → don't change anything. Let it compound.
If they've stopped → ask why (calmly, not as a sales pitch). The honest answer tells you what the GPT is missing.
