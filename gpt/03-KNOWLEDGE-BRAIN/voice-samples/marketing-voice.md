## Sample 1 — Instagram carousel (philosophy beat — Traffic Light Zones)

```
[Slide 1 — hook]
Read the ball like a traffic light.

[Slide 2 — frame]
Every ball coming at you is one of three colors.
Most errors happen when players treat a red ball like a green one.

[Slide 3 — RED]
Red. You're stretched, late, or deep behind the baseline.
This is not the time to go for a winner.
Hit it high over the net, deep to the middle. Buy time. Recover.

[Slide 4 — YELLOW]
Yellow. It's a neutral ball. Neither player has the advantage.
Hit to the biggest target — usually big crosscourt.
Stay patient. Wait for something better.

[Slide 5 — GREEN]
Green. You got the short ball. Feet are set. You're balanced.
Now you attack — but to the open space, not by swinging harder.

[Slide 6 — try it]
Before every shot this week, ask one question:
red, yellow, or green?
You'll be surprised how many errors disappear when the decision matches the situation.

[Slide 7 — soft CTA]
Frameworks like this run through every group class on our courts.
Spring runs through June 13. Free trial in any group class.

[Caption]
A framework we teach at every level. The shot was fine. The decision was wrong — that's where most points are lost. One question before each ball: red, yellow, or green? Try it this weekend.

#tennis #lagunabeach #junior tennis #adulttennis
```

*Why this works:* The carousel earns the swipe. Slide 1 is a hook, not a headline; slides 2–6 develop one idea (color = decision rule) with concrete instruction; slide 7 ends on practice, not on "sign up." The caption summarizes the same beat in a sentence — useful for the user who never opens the carousel. Founder voice: calm, specific, no exclamation marks, no hyperbole.

A new player — first time ever holding a racquet — kept apologizing every time she missed. After about ten minutes, the coach stopped feeding and said one sentence: "Missing is the practice. The misses are how the brain finds the shot."

## Sample 4 — Newsletter founder note (Sunday weekly — Desirable Difficulty)

```
EYEBROW: SPRING 2026
Headline: The 70% Rule.
Subhead: Why the right difficulty level matters more than more reps.

Hey [first name],

There's a concept in motor learning research called desirable difficulty — and it explains something most tennis players get wrong about practice.

If you're hitting every ball in during a drill, you're not improving. You're just reinforcing what you already know. And if you're missing everything, you're just frustrated. Neither one moves the needle.

The sweet spot — where learning actually happens fastest — is around 70% success. Hard enough that you have to focus. Easy enough that you can feel yourself getting better. That tension between challenge and confidence is where real growth lives.

This is how we calibrate every drill at LBTA. We adjust targets, distances, and rules until the success rate feels like "I can do this, but I have to concentrate." If kids are nailing everything, we make the target smaller or add a constraint. If they're struggling, we pull back. The 70% zone is the goal.

— Andrew
Founder & Academy Director · Laguna Beach Tennis Academy
```

*Why this works:* Lands one principle in 200 words. Names the research (motor learning, desirable difficulty) without name-dropping a paper. Translates the principle into a concrete coaching practice ("we adjust targets, distances, and rules"). Tone is teacher-in-confidence — not a brand voice, a person voice. No CTA — the value IS the CTA.

## Sample 5 — Newsletter founder note (different week — One Cue Word)

```
EYEBROW: A NOTE FROM ANDREW
Headline: One word.
Subhead: The simplest mental tool that actually works.

Hey [first name],

The difference between a focused player and a scattered one isn't talent. It's not experience. It's not fitness.

It's how many things they're thinking about.

A scattered player walks up to the baseline thinking about their toss, their grip, their footwork, the wind, the last point they lost, what they're having for dinner. Their brain is full of noise. Every bit of noise steals attention from the one thing that actually matters: the next ball.

A focused player is thinking about one word.

Before every point, choose one word. That's your entire focus for that point.

"Feet." — if your movement has been lazy, this pulls your attention to your split step and recovery.
"Deep." — if you've been hitting short, this anchors your aim past the service line.
"Breathe." — if you're tight and rushing between points, this slows you down.

One word. Not three. Not a strategy. Not a swing thought.

— Andrew
```

*Why this works:* Same surface as Sample 4, totally different rhythm. Short paragraphs, near-poetic structure, list of three concrete cue words. Demonstrates the GPT should not template the newsletter — every founder note has its own shape, sized to the idea. The "feet / deep / breathe" trio is the kind of specific that's hard to fake.

## Sample 7 — Blog post opening (first 100 words)

```
Movement before strokes: why footwork comes first

Most people arrive thinking about the racket: grip, swing path, topspin. Those matter. But the ball almost always rewards the player who arrives on balance with time — and punishes the one who reaches or leans.

The contact point is earned in the feet.

If you are late to the ball, your swing tightens. If you are jammed, you lift or guide. If you are stretched wide, you sacrifice shape for survival. None of that is a "mental" problem first; it is spacing.
```

*Why this works:* No SEO sludge ("Looking for the best tennis academy in Laguna Beach? Look no further!"). Opens with the contrarian beat, lands the principle, then proves it with three concrete failure modes (late, jammed, stretched). Zero throat-clearing. The H2 is a sentence — "The contact point is earned in the feet" — that doubles as a thesis.

## Sample 8 — Blog post middle section (philosophy beat in long form)

```
Drills are not choreography.

Footwork drills should look boring on paper and feel honest on court. The goal is not to memorize a dance; it is to make neutral and defensive positions familiar before pressure arrives. When movement is automatic, attention can go to decision-making — height, depth, speed, and when to step in.

What parents notice when it clicks.

You might not see a different swing in week one. You often see a calmer player: fewer rushed swings, better spacing on mid-court balls, and cleaner preparation on serves and returns. That is the movement foundation showing up before the stroke "looks different" on video.

Across adult and junior training, we keep the same principle: athletic literacy first, then craft. Competitive players still hit thousands of balls — but the reps assume you know how to arrive.
```

*Why this works:* Two complete beats in one section, each with a thesis sentence. Re-orients parents — the result they're looking for ("a calmer player") is the early signal, not the swing change. The phrase "athletic literacy first, then craft" is brand language that compounds across surfaces. This is what blog-length founder voice looks like: instructive, never preachy.

## Sample 10 — Partner outreach email (cold)

```
Subject: Tennis programming for [School Name] families

Hi [First Name],

I run Laguna Beach Tennis Academy — we operate as the official recreation tennis partner of the City of Laguna Beach across three sites (Moulton Meadows, LBHS, Alta Laguna). About 40% of the families we coach come through your district, and a few have asked whether we'd ever offer something on-site.

Two things I wanted to put on your radar in case useful: (1) we run after-school clinics at our public sites that already work with school dismissal times, and (2) we have a small fund — the LBTA Scholarship — that quietly covers a kid's full season when a family asks. No application form. Just a private note from a counselor or coach.

If a 15-minute call is useful — even just to know we exist for the families you talk to — happy to set one up.

— Andrew
Founder & Academy Director · Laguna Beach Tennis Academy
(XXX) XXX-XXXX
```

*Why this works:* Opens with one earned reason the recipient should care ("40% of the families we coach come through your district"), not with a pitch. Drops two specific value items the partner can use immediately (after-school timing, scholarship fund). The ask is calibrated to the relationship: "15 minutes, even just to know we exist." No deck, no PDF, no follow-up sequence implied.

## Sample 11 — Partner outreach email (warm follow-up)

```
Subject: Re: Tennis programming for [School Name] families

Hi [First Name],

No rush at all on this — just wanted to send one note. We had a family from your school's 4th grade enroll a kid this week who's been on financial aid through your office. The mom told us the counselor mentioned us in passing — that's the only reason she reached out.

That's the entire reason for the partner conversation. Not a program to sign, not a deck to review. Just making sure the families you already know about know we exist.

Whenever works for you — or never if it never makes sense.

— Andrew
```

*Why this works:* The follow-up doesn't escalate, it de-escalates. "Whenever works for you — or never if it never makes sense" is a real LBTA voice line — calm, no leverage, no pressure. Tells one specific story (a family enrolled because the counselor mentioned it), which IS the case for partnership without ever saying "the case for partnership."

## Sample 13 — Enrollment email sequence (one of 5 — Inside a Session)

```
Subject: 60 minutes — what a class actually looks like

Hey [first name],

The biggest barrier to trying something new isn't the cost. It's the unknown.

What happens when my kid shows up? Will they know anyone? Will they be the worst one there? What if they don't like it?

I hear some version of this from almost every new parent. So I want to take the mystery out of it. Here's what a real 60-minute Red Ball session looks like — the kind your 5- or 6-year-old would walk into.

Minutes 0–10: Warm-up games. Not stretching. Not laps. Tag with tennis balls. Relay races between cones. By minute 10, every kid is smiling and slightly out of breath.

Minutes 10–25: Skill game. Coach sets up a game with specific rules that develop a specific skill. If the theme is "rally tolerance," the game might be: keep the ball going 5 times to earn a point. No lecture on grip. Just a target, a rule, and a reason to try.

Minutes 25–55: Rally games, then real points. Champions and challengers. The energy goes up. This is where kids learn to compete — to win with grace and lose without falling apart.

Minutes 55–60: Parent handoff. Coach is available for a quick word. A sentence or two on what they worked on and what went well.

No standing in line waiting for a turn. No 20-minute lectures on technique. Just 60 minutes of structured, purposeful play that happens to build real tennis skills along the way.

Seeing it is better than reading about it. Come try a class.

— Andrew
```

*Why this works:* Reads standalone — even if it's email 4 of 5, you don't need email 1–3 to follow it. Names the real anxiety ("Will they be the worst one there?") in the parent's actual voice, then dissolves it with concrete minute-by-minute detail. The CTA at the bottom is one sentence and earned by the 250 words above it.

## Anti-samples (do NOT include — what off-voice looks like)

- *"Don't miss our amazing Spring Break Camp! Limited spots — register NOW!"* — exclamation point + manufactured urgency + "amazing" = forbidden words list violation.
- *"At LBTA, we believe in helping every child reach their full potential through world-class coaching."* — generic, "world-class," doesn't pass the "did Andrew actually write this?" test.
- *"🎾 Calling all tennis families! 🎾 Don't sleep on summer camp registration! ⭐⭐⭐"* — emoji-stuffed, exclamation-heavy, no information density.
- Drafts the GPT wrote that Andrew approved without revision — those are GPT echoes, not human samples. They'll loop.
- Anything featuring a kid by full name, a parent's full email, or a phone number that isn't `(949) 534-0457`.
